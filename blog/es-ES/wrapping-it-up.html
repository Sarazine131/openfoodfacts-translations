Probablemente ya ha visto códigos como "EMB 56251e" o "EN 38.012.001 CE" en etiquetas y envases de alimentos. Y probablemente no les prestaste atención. And yet, these codes - called "packager codes" for the first and "European sanitary stamps" for the second - can teach us a lot about the products we buy and eat!



Un código de embalaje o sello sanitario = una empresa

El código de empaquetado identifica a la empresa que envasa el producto alimentario. Esta puede ser la empresa que hizo el producto, o la empresa que lo importaba y lo empaquetaba.

It must be mentioned when the name and address of the company that prepared the product is not clearly indicated on the packaging. Packaging codes are thus found on products where only the consumer service address of the brand is included.

The European sanitary stamp or European sanitary approval number identifies the companies that produce or process animal and animal food products.


You can now in which commune the product is manufactured, transformed (or at least packed)

Los primeros 2 dígitos del código "EMB 56251e" identifican el departamento, aquí 56, Morbihan.
Los siguientes 3 dígitos identifican el municipio donde se encuentra la empresa. But it is not the postal code, it is a number attributed to the commune by the INSEE. El número 251 del departamento francés de Morbihan es Theix.

To these figures may be added a letter that differentiates food companies when several are established in the same municipality.

The codes of the form "FR 38.012.001 CE" correspond to the European approval numbers. Las 2 primeras letras identifican al país. And we find the number of the department and the number of the town in the first 2 groups of numbers. The last digit group identifies the company.

A great interest of the packager code or sanitary stamp is therefore to know which city a product comes from. This can be interesting to focus on local products and avoid products that must cross the whole of France. This also allows to see what types of products are manufactured in a municipality or department.

Since today, you will find the town and the department next to the packager codes on the Open Food Facts product sheets. (which makes it possible to check that the ham of "Aoste" brand is indeed packaged in Aoste (the small commune of Isère, not the capital of Val d'Aoste in Italy))

See which products of different brands are manufactured in the same factory

Another very interesting use of packaging codes is to cross-reference them across the entire Open Food Facts product base . Cabe señalar que algunas empresas fabrican productos para varias marcas.

So our company in Theix code EMB 56251e prepares as well boxes of green beans for the D'Aucy brand as well as boxes of lentils prepared for Cora .



Another example: a factory in Normandy in Condé sur Vire that produces Elle & Vire cream and Carrefour half-salt butter.

The fact that two similar products (for example, two boxes of lentils of different brands) are prepared or packaged in the same plant does not necessarily mean that the two products are identical. Para asegurarse, puede comparar las hojas de cada producto para verificar si los ingredientes y la composición nutricional también son idénticos.

Entonces, ¿vamos a envolverlo?

Espero que nunca miren estos códigos de la misma manera (¡o mejor dicho, empiecen a mirarlos!), y que juntos podremos hacer interesantes y quizás sorprendentes descubrimientos, ya que identificamos todos los productos envasados en diferentes talleres y fábricas.

The next time you add a product in the Open Food Facts product database, do not forget to check if the packaging has a packaging code: it will be very useful!
